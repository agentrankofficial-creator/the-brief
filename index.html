The other in San Francisco — a CMS configuration error at Anthropic exposed 3,000 internal documents about Claude Mythos, an AI that can autonomously find and exploit zero-day vulnerabilities in every major OS and browser. The structural damage in both cases is permanent, regardless of whether ceasefire holds or the leak gets cleaned up. Every company not on it now knows exactly where it stands.

What nobody is connecting: India's PFBR achieving criticality on April 6 — barely noticed amid the noise — is the most structurally important energy story of this entire cycle, precisely because it's the only one that's unambiguously constructive. While the world scrambles over Hormuz, India quietly demonstrated it can breed its own nuclear fuel. Bhabha's 70-year plan, one reactor, and suddenly India's energy dependence story shifts from "permanent import dependency" to "potentially sovereign on a 20-year horizon." That matters for every business making long-term India bets.

For Canada, the picture is split: WTI at $91 is excellent for Alberta oil sands — Hormuz disruption is a tailwind for Canadian energy producers. But BRP's $500M tariff hit, soft CAD (~0.72), and a BoC holding at 2.25% tell us Canada's non-resource economy is still absorbing structural headwinds. Rogers' rumored MLSE IPO is the most interesting Canadian capital markets story in years — and the AI trade suggestions controversy at the Maple Leafs front office tells you exactly where the hedge fund playbook is heading next.

My forward call: The Glasswing partner list functions as the de facto "security tier 1" index for the next decade. Watch for M&A targeting conspicuous absences. The exclusion of Cloudflare cost it double-digit market value in four days — now imagine what happens when the first Mythos-equivalent attack hits a non-Glasswing financial institution.

India · Energy · Nuclear ↑ Long-term · Decades NOTE
India's Fast Breeder Reactor Achieves Criticality — Bhabha's 70-Year Nuclear Plan Clears Its Most Critical Hurdle, 15 Years Late

On April 6, India's 500 MWe Prototype Fast Breeder Reactor (PFBR) at Kalpakkam, Tamil Nadu achieved first criticality at 8:25 PM — the self-sustaining nuclear chain reaction that proves Stage 2 of Homi Bhabha's three-stage nuclear programme is technically viable. The reactor uses plutonium-MOX fuel cooled by liquid sodium and is 15 years behind schedule, with a cost of ₹8,181 crore (~$3.9B PPP) vs. an original estimate of ₹3,492 crore. Russia has the only other operating commercial fast breeder. Japan, France, and the U.S. all tried and failed. India succeeded because Stage 2 is non-negotiable for Stage 3 — the thorium endgame that would unlock India's massive domestic reserves.

SO WHAT (India): This doesn't solve India's energy crisis tomorrow — commercial operations are years away, thorium Stage 3 is 15–20 years out. But it validates the strategic bet. India's dependence on Canadian uranium and Gulf oil just became slightly less existential over the 2035+ horizon. Every long-term India business model should revise energy cost assumptions upward for the far end of the runway.
SO WHAT (Canada): India is a major customer for Canadian uranium under the India-Canada Nuclear Cooperation Agreement. If India's thorium programme eventually succeeds at scale, Canadian uranium export demand from India could soften over 20 years — a very slow-moving risk for Canadian uranium mining.
ACTION: Research India's nuclear supply chain beneficiaries: L&T (nuclear engineering and construction), BHEL (nuclear manufacturing), NFC (Nuclear Fuel Complex). These are undervalued long-duration infrastructure plays. Watch for India's announcement of PFBR-2 and PFBR-3 — 4 more units are planned.
💡 India's PFBR was 15 years late and 2.3x over budget. The IPFM notes that fast breeders globally have proven expensive and difficult to operate at scale. Criticality is a lab proof-of-concept; commercial viability at low cost is a different challenge entirely, and Stage 3 thorium reactors have never been built anywhere.

The three-stage plan: Stage 1 (current) — PHWRs using natural uranium, producing plutonium as byproduct. Stage 2 (just validated) — Fast Breeder Reactors using that plutonium to produce more fuel than consumed. Stage 3 — Thorium-based reactors using bred material to unlock India's reserves of 25% of global thorium.

Why the world failed and India didn't: Japan's Monju shut after a sodium leak fire in 1995. The U.S.'s Clinch River project was abandoned due to cost overruns. France's Superphenix struggled with utilization. India stayed the course because FBRs are non-negotiable for Stage 3 — there is no alternative pathway to energy sovereignty given India's uranium scarcity.

The Hormuz connection (Finshots): India depends on multi-billion dollar uranium import agreements with Canada. Fast breeders change that equation over time by producing more fuel than consumed, turning scarce plutonium into abundance. The PFBR is Stage 2's Hormuz immunity shot — it just takes 15 more years to administer.

THE LONG GAME

Slow-Burn Trend Worth Tracking
The emergence of "Claudeonomics" — measuring AI token spend as a percentage of payroll — is the most important organizational metric you've never tracked. Meta monitors it. Jensen Huang recommends matching half your salary equivalent in compute tokens per month. Dave Blundin targets 1:1 AI-to-payroll cost ratio by year-end. This framework redefines what a productive organization looks like: companies that achieve high AI-to-payroll ratios have marginal costs approaching zero on white-collar functions; laggards carry full human payroll on tasks machines now do cheaper. The barbell will widen through 2030. Watch India's IT services sector especially — the BPO and outsourcing model employing millions is being disrupted simultaneously from the top (enterprise AI replacing workflows) and the bottom (AI-native SaaS products eliminating the need for the outsourced service entirely). Infosys, Wipro, TCS face the most complex navigation: their clients want them to deploy the very AI that replaces the labor the business model is built on. Canada's professional services sector — legal, accounting, financial advisory — faces the same compression. The transition window where human domain expertise commands a premium over AI-augmented competitors is roughly 2025–2030. After that, the premium accrues entirely to AI orchestration skill, not domain knowledge alone. Start measuring your AI-to-payroll ratio today.
