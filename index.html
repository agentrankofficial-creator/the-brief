Hormuz Blockade Goes Live: US Navy Begins Policing Iranian Port Traffic as Peace Talks Collapse
CENTCOM confirms the US naval blockade of Iranian ports began at 14:00 GMT today, blocking all maritime traffic entering and exiting Iranian coastal areas. Oil surges 8% above $100/bbl. Trump threatens to intercept any vessel that paid Iran a transit toll. Iran calls it "piracy."
▶ Expand for analysis
Analysis

The shift from announcement to execution is the critical inflection. On Sunday, after JD Vance failed to reach a deal in Pakistan, Trump declared the blockade. By Monday morning, CENTCOM operationalized it. The scope is narrower than feared — only Iranian ports, not all strait traffic — but still represents the most aggressive US naval action in the Gulf since 1988's Operation Praying Mantis.

The sticking points in Pakistan: Iran demanded continued control of Hormuz toll collection and refused to surrender enriched uranium stockpile. The US demanded both. Neither side blinked.

SO WHAT

This is no longer a diplomatic signal — it's kinetic. Oil will likely stay above $100 until either a new round of talks is announced or Iran retaliates. Every asset class is now pricing in an extended confrontation. India faces acute pressure: jet fuel costs have already nearly doubled, and INR is weakening.

WHAT TO DO
  • Re-evaluate energy-exposed positions. Oil & gas producers benefit; airlines, logistics, paint, and chemical companies face margin compression.
  • Watch gold — it dipped on strong dollar but typically rallies on sustained conflict.
  • Track UK mine-sweeper deployment as signal of coalition-building.
  • Indian investors: defensive sectors (pharma, FMCG, IT) outperform in prolonged conflict scenarios.
CONTRARIAN VIEW

Markets may be overreacting. The blockade targets only Iranian ports, not the full strait. ~21% of global oil passes through Hormuz, but most of it goes to non-Iranian ports which remain open. If Iran doesn't retaliate militarily, this could be a slow squeeze rather than a crisis. Traders who view the talks breakdown as "temporary" may limit the selloff.

Deep Dive

Timeline of collapse: The two-week ceasefire triggered a relief rally last week. Talks in Islamabad were meant to formalize it into a lasting deal. Vance led the US delegation; Iran's sticking points were Hormuz sovereignty and uranium stockpile. After marathon sessions, both sides left the door open for further diplomacy but no date was set.

Market mechanics: WTI jumped 8.54% to $104.82/bbl. Brent gained 7.27% to $102.51. Japan's bond yield hit a 29-year high as energy import costs spike. US inflation surged in March (Friday's data) — war-driven gas prices the culprit. This week's earnings season (Goldman reports Monday) will be parsed for conflict-impact commentary.

Iran's response: Iran's armed forces called the blockade "an illegal act amounting to piracy." Trump also threatened China with 50% tariffs if it provides military assistance to Iran — US intelligence suggests China may have shipped missiles to Iran, per NYT. Beijing denied this.

Ray Dalio's framework: In a widely-shared post, Dalio argues we're at Step 9 of a 13-step world war cycle — multi-theater conflicts across continents. He compares the period to 1913–14 and 1938–39, puts odds of a new major military conflict within 5 years at >50%, with US-China over Taiwan peaking around 2028. Markets, he says, are "badly mispricing" this environment.

Analysis

EU Commission President von der Leyen immediately celebrated. JD Vance had campaigned for Orbán last week — his defeat is personally embarrassing for the US VP. Analysis

The AI disruption of software has moved from "someday" to "this earnings season." This is particularly relevant for Indian IT services — Kotak flags Anthropic's Mythos as a disruption risk to India's IT growth. TCS CEO Krithivasan pushes back, calling it an opportunity, but the market is nervous. Figma at $7.9B enterprise value is now less than half the $20B Adobe planned to pay in 2022.

WHAT TO DO
  • Revisit any SaaS holdings — especially collaboration tools (Asana, Notion) and design tools (Figma) most exposed to AI substitution.
  • Cybersecurity may not be the safe haven investors assumed — AI firms are entering the space directly.
  • Indian IT: watch TCS and Infosys earnings closely for AI deal commentary. TCS reported record $40.7B TCV in FY26.
Sources: The Information, ET AI, ETtech Morning Dispatch
Deep Dive

The numbers: ServiceNow -8%, Snowflake -8%, Salesforce -3.5%, Palo Alto -6.7%, CrowdStrike -4% in a single Friday session. Figma's enterprise value ($7.9B) is less than half what Adobe was willing to pay pre-antitrust block. Asana is down 60% YTD.

Nvidia Backs RISC-V Chip Startup SiFive at $3.65B — Open-Source Silicon Enters the AI Data Center
SiFive raised $400M in an oversubscribed round led by Atreides Management with Nvidia as a notable new investor. The company builds CPUs on RISC-V, a royalty-free open architecture, and is integrating with Nvidia's CUDA and NVLink ecosystem.
▶ Expand for analysis
Analysis

Nvidia isn't just making a financial bet — it's integrating SiFive's RISC-V designs into its own ecosystem (CUDA software, NVLink Fusion rack servers). This is a strategic flanking move against Intel and AMD. If RISC-V CPUs can pair seamlessly with Nvidia GPUs in AI data centers, it creates a new architecture stack entirely outside ARM/x86. SiFive's business model mirrors ARM — licensing designs rather than manufacturing chips — giving it capital-light economics.

SO WHAT

This is the open-source moment for silicon. With Apollo, T. Rowe Price, and D1 Capital also backing the round, institutional conviction is high. The last raise was $175M in 2022, making this a significant step up in ambition. Watch for data center adoption announcements in H2 2026.
