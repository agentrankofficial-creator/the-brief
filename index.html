Suhail Seth · April 17, 2026

The Iran ceasefire expires April 21, and nobody seriously believes it holds. Trump says the war is "very close to over" — which, translated from presidential optimism, means: talks are happening but the nuclear question remains unresolved, and Iran knows its leverage disappears the moment ships start moving through Hormuz again. Today the US Navy blockade entered Day 4, with 14 vessels turned back. The strait isn't opening this week.

But here's what I keep coming back to: even if a deal gets signed Monday, the world has just learned something it cannot unlearn. For seven weeks, 21% of global seaborne oil trade simply stopped moving. The geopolitical insurance premium on Middle Eastern energy is now permanent and priced in. And two of my businesses sit on either side of that pricing.

In India, the rupee is at ₹93/$1 — down nearly 9% year-on-year — not because the economy is weak (it's growing 6.5%, fastest among major economies) but because we're an oil importer with a strengthening dollar headwind. The IMF just dropped India to 6th in nominal GDP rankings, behind the UK. That's not a real economic event — it's arithmetic — but it's a political and narrative event that the government will have to manage carefully. The real cost is invisible: every rupee of depreciation adds to the import bill, and with oil above $90/barrel, that bill is punishing.

In Canada, the story is almost the opposite. The Hormuz crisis is Canada's clearest strategic energy opportunity in a generation. LNG Canada Phase 2 FID is on the table this year. Asian buyers — Japan, South Korea, India — are lining up. And yet Canada is simultaneously in an "entrepreneurial drought" — closing more businesses than it opens, worst since the pandemic. The country that should be saying yes to the world is saying no to its own entrepreneurs. I think this is the contradiction Ottawa needs to confront directly.

My forward call: this ceasefire extension buys 2–3 weeks. If talks collapse again, WTI crosses $100 and stays there. In that scenario, India's current account deficit widens by $15–20B annually, the rupee tests ₹96, and Canadian LNG FIDs become unstoppable. If a deal lands, oil retraces to $75–80, Indian markets rally 4–6%, and the Canadian LNG window narrows. Watch April 21 like it's the most important date in energy markets this year — because it is.

The fragile US-Iran truce brokered through Pakistan is set to expire April 21. Marathon talks in Islamabad last weekend broke down over Iran's nuclear demands. SO WHAT:
India: India imports ~85% of its oil. With the rupee already at ₹93/$1 and WTI near $94, the combined FX + commodity shock is devastating to the current account. Every $10/barrel above $80 costs India ~$8–10B/year in extra import bills. The rupee is the transmission mechanism — and it's already cracking.

Canada: Alberta crude (Western Canadian Select) is not Hormuz-linked. Canadian oil and LNG have a structural premium right now — no maritime choke-point exposure. This is the best demand environment in Canadian energy history for expanding export capacity. The ceasefire is actually bad for Canadian energy bulls in the short term — a deal sends oil to $75 fast.
ACTION: April 21 is the binary trigger. If ceasefire collapses: WTI breaks $100 — hedge Indian operations' energy exposure, accelerate any conversations about Canadian LNG FID partnerships. If deal lands: Indian equities rally hard, take profit on any energy positions. Set a calendar reminder for April 20 — pre-position the day before.
💭 Bear case for Canadian energy: A deal, even a fragile one, collapses oil 15–20% overnight. WCS (Western Canadian Select) follows. The FID window reopens only if instability returns. Don't over-rotate on a single geopolitical event.

Even if a ceasefire holds, full shipping normalization takes weeks — mine clearance operations alone are complex.

India Slips to 6th in IMF GDP Rankings — It's Currency Math, Not Economic Failure

The IMF's April 2026 World Economic Outlook puts India's nominal GDP at $4.15 trillion — behind the UK ($4.26T) and Japan ($4.38T), dropping India to 6th place. This lands with political force: politicians have been promising India becomes the 4th-largest economy. The rupee's slide from ₹84.6/$ in 2024 to ₹88.5 in 2025 (and now ₹93+) is the mechanical cause — India's GDP grew ~9% in rupee terms, but dollar-conversion erased the gain. A Feb 2026 base-year revision by MoSPI knocked a further ~4% off the nominal figure.

SO WHAT in India: This is a narrative crisis more than an economic one — India is still the world's fastest-growing major economy at 6.5% real GDP. But the optics matter. Expect the government to redouble rhetoric around "India at 3rd by 2030," and possibly accelerate RBI intervention to support the rupee. For businesses operating in INR with dollar costs, the FX headwind is very real.

SO WHAT in Canada: The dollar's global strength (which is hurting INR) is also keeping CAD subdued at ~$0.738 USD. But Canada is a commodity exporter — a strong USD + high oil prices is actually net positive for CAD revenues denominated in USD.
ACTION: If you have INR-denominated assets with dollar-priced inputs (energy, electronics, capital goods), review currency hedging strategy urgently. The rupee at ₹95 is not impossible by Q3 if Hormuz stays closed. Dollar-cost averaging into USD-denominated assets from INR is sensible here.
💭 Counter: India's real GDP growth at 6.5% remains the fastest globally. PPP rankings (where India is 3rd) tell a different story. Nominal GDP ranking is a snapshot of exchange-rate math, not economic strength. Don't mistake the score for the game.
Read original (BusinessToday) →

The base-year revision effect: When MoSPI shifted the base year from 2011-12 to 2022-23, it revised down India's nominal GDP by ~4%. This is a statistical correction, not an economic one — but it compounds with the currency effect to create a double-whammy in the IMF rankings.

The UK comparison: The UK economy is not actually growing faster than India — UK real GDP growth is forecast at ~1.2% for 2026. India beat the UK purely because the pound strengthened against the dollar while the rupee weakened. This is entirely a currency story.

Path back to 5th: For India to reclaim 5th, the rupee needs to stabilize below ₹87/$. Given current Hormuz dynamics, that seems optimistic before H2 2026 at the earliest.

The Rupee at ₹93: RBI Is Fighting, But Oil + Dollar Is Winning

The USD/INR rate touched ₹93.37 this week — a three-week low — as rising oil prices from Hormuz fears and broadly stronger dollar sentiment combined. The rupee is down 8.94% over the past 12 months. RBI has intervened — pushing lenders to unwind offshore arbitrage positions — and temporarily stabilized around ₹93.10. But the structural pressures (oil import bill, capital outflows, Trump tariffs on Indian goods) haven't eased.

SO WHAT in India: For any business with USD-priced inputs — technology, energy, capital equipment — this is a 9% cost increase versus last year. Import-heavy sectors (electronics, pharma raw materials) are getting squeezed. Export-oriented businesses (IT services, specialty chemicals) get a tailwind — their dollar revenues buy more rupees.

SO WHAT in Canada: The same dollar strength that's hurting INR is keeping CAD range-bound at $0.738. But Canada's current account benefits from oil exports priced in USD — a strong dollar actually boosts Alberta's revenue in CAD terms.
ACTION: Businesses with significant USD payables should consider locking in forward contracts at current levels before April 21 (Hormuz deadline). If ceasefire collapses, rupee could test ₹96 within days. If deal lands, cover slowly — don't over-hedge on the downside.
💭 The RBI has $650B+ in reserves. It has firepower to defend the rupee. The structural story for INR over 3–5 years remains positive as India's current account improves. This is cyclical pain, not structural rot.

The triple-squeeze: India faces three simultaneous pressures on the rupee: (1) oil import bill surge from Hormuz; (2) capital outflows as US rates stay elevated; (3) Trump tariff uncertainty, which has kept FDI flows subdued. Any one of these is manageable. All three together is why the RBI is running hard to stand still.

The arbitrage play the RBI is targeting: Offshore NDF (non-deliverable forward) markets were trading the rupee at a significant discount to onshore rates, creating an arbitrage that amplified selling pressure. The RBI's intervention to unwind these positions is a known playbook — it worked in 2022–23 as well.

💭 Survivorship bias: we're seeing the winners' playbooks, not the graveyard of companies that tried "industry convergence" with AI and failed expensively. Not every firm can or should expand beyond their sector. Capital discipline still matters.

One Year After Liberation Day: SCOTUS Killed IEEPA Tariffs, But Trump's New 10% Global Tariff Is Already Here — And Now India Is Under Section 301 Investigation

In February 2026, the US Supreme Court (6-3) struck down Trump's "Liberation Day" reciprocal tariffs as exceeding presidential authority under IEEPA. Within hours, Trump signed a new 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974. Now, USTR has initiated Section 301 investigations into structural excess capacity in manufacturing across 16 countries including India, with hearings scheduled for April 28, 2026. The trade war isn't over — it just found a new legal vehicle.

SO WHAT in India: India is now formally under a Section 301 investigation for "structural excess capacity." This is early-stage — hearings are April 28 — but it signals that US-India trade tension is not easing. Indian export sectors (pharmaceuticals, auto components, steel) need to monitor this closely. The $165B+ trade realignment already underway is accelerating.

SO WHAT in Canada: Canada-US trade is governed separately under USMCA, which provides some insulation from the 10% global tariff. But the CFIB data (see Canadian File) shows Canadian businesses are already under enormous cost pressure — any further US trade friction could be the margin call that tips more closures.
ACTION: If you have Indian businesses with US export exposure, get trade counsel involved before April 28 hearings. The Section 301 process can escalate to tariff recommendations within 12 months. This is not a drill.
💭 The Section 122 tariff (10% global) is legally weaker than IEEPA and could face its own constitutional challenge. Trade lawyers are already filing briefs. The administration's tariff tools keep getting whittled down — the actual implementation may be far messier than the announcements suggest.
Read original (SCOTUSblog) → Tariff tracker →

The IEEPA ruling in context: The Court held that "regulate importation" in IEEPA does not include the power to impose tariffs — that's a congressional power under Article I. This is the most significant constraint on executive trade power in decades. The Penn-Wharton estimate is that IEEPA-based tariff collections were running at $175–179B annually before the ruling.

What Section 301 means: Unlike IEEPA, Section 301 of the Trade Act explicitly authorizes tariffs in response to unfair foreign trade practices. It's the same authority used for the original China tariffs in 2018. The investigations are sector-specific and can take 12–18 months to resolve, but the process itself creates business uncertainty that suppresses investment.

Nature Study: Human Scientists Still Beat AI Agents on Complex, Novel Tasks

A Nature paper published this week finds that while AI agents match or exceed human scientists on known benchmarks, human experts still "trounce" the best AI on genuinely complex, novel tasks — the kind where no training data exists. The implication: AI is a productivity multiplier for research, not (yet) an autonomous research generator. For any business investing in R&D with AI assistance, the framework should be "AI-augmented human" not "AI-replaces researcher."

SO WHAT: The Stanford AI Index data (50%+ on Humanity's Last Exam) and this Nature finding coexist in a way that's important to understand: benchmark performance ≠ real-world novel problem solving. The benchmark games are partly training set contamination. Don't make capital allocation decisions based on benchmarks alone.
ACTION: Test your AI tools on genuinely novel problems in your domain — not textbook cases. The answer you get will tell you more about actual capability than any published benchmark.
💭 The Nature study uses a specific definition of "novel" — tasks where training data is unlikely to exist. As the training data frontier advances, today's "novel" becomes tomorrow's benchmark. The lead time may be 12–24 months, not permanent.

♟ The Long Game
The Hormuz crisis will eventually resolve — either through a deal or an escalation that forces resolution. But the world that emerges will have permanently repriced geopolitical risk on energy infrastructure. Every company, every government, every pension fund that thought "Middle Eastern energy is reliably cheap" has been proven wrong in a 7-week window. The slow-burn consequence is a decade-long shift in energy infrastructure investment: undersea cables, pipelines that avoid choke-points, nuclear (which L&T and others are already betting on), and LNG routes with zero Hormuz exposure. Canada is the most obvious geographic beneficiary of this repricing. But India needs to make a strategic choice: remain exposed to Hormuz risk as a major oil importer, or spend the next 10 years diversifying energy sources toward domestic renewables, nuclear, and Canadian LNG. The economics of that choice are not being made with the urgency the Hormuz reality demands. This is the story I will be watching for the next 3 years.
