And then, within roughly 24 hours, Iran closed it again because the US refused to lift its naval blockade. That whiplash is the story. Iran has just demonstrated that the Strait of Hormuz is no longer a shipping lane — it is a permanent geopolitical instrument, as reliable a deterrent as a nuclear weapon. Fortune called it exactly right: Iran now holds a card that works "almost like a nuclear deterrent."

Here's the cross-reference nobody is making: while Trump is laser-focused on Iran, Canada's PM Carney has been quietly reaching out to China. US Commerce Secretary Lutnick noticed — that's why his "they suck" eruption was specifically about Canada's China outreach, not just the wait-it-out trade strategy. I think Carney is playing a smarter game than he's getting credit for. By diversifying toward China and the EU, he's reducing Canada's leverage problem before the USMCA review lands. Lutnick's rage is confirmation that it's working.

The India-Canada mirror here is striking. India is a massive oil importer — every $10/barrel drop in WTI is roughly $12B in annual import savings. Canada is a major oil producer — the WTI crash is tearing up TSX energy stocks. Two countries I operate in, same macro event, opposite outcomes. When the same dollar-denominated oil price move is simultaneously good and bad for your two biggest markets, that's when you need to think carefully about which side of every trade you're on.

My forward call: the Hormuz situation will not resolve cleanly. Iran has learned the lesson — opening and closing the strait is its most powerful bargaining chip. Expect the strait to be weaponized every time Iran needs leverage in any future negotiation. Structure your energy exposure accordingly.

Geopolitics ⚡ Immediate + structural ACT
Iran opened the Strait of Hormuz — then closed it again in 24 hours. The world just watched the most important chokepoint become a permanent geopolitical weapon.

Iran announced Friday that it had opened the Strait of Hormuz to all commercial shipping, citing the US-brokered ceasefire that paused Israel's Lebanon campaign. Markets exploded: WTI crude plunged 11.4% to $83.85 — its largest single-day drop since the crisis began — from a peak of $118/barrel. Airline stocks surged 6%+. The S&P 500 hit its third consecutive all-time high close. Bitcoin ripped to $78k. Then on April 18, Iran closed it again after the US refused to lift its naval blockade. An estimated 135 million barrels of oil remain stranded in tankers in the Persian Gulf, per Bloomberg Intelligence.

US-Iran talks are set for Pakistan. Trump says a deal is "within a day or two" — he also said Iran agreed to suspend its nuclear program indefinitely, then denied any money would change hands. The gap between what Trump says in public and what's actually on the table appears vast. Trump told Axios he'd consider a "peace deal soon," but the market has now been burned twice by premature optimism.

SO WHAT IN INDIA: India imports ~87% of its crude. Every $10/bbl drop = ~$12B in annual import savings and significant CAD compression. The rupee (₹92.61/USD) would likely strengthen 1-2% on a durable Hormuz resolution. Indian airline and refinery stocks could see a significant re-rating Monday when markets reopen. SO WHAT IN CANADA: Canada is a major oil producer — TSX was already red (-0.30%) while the US surged. A sustained WTI drop to $75-80 would hammer Canadian energy companies, provincial royalty revenues (especially Alberta), and put downward pressure on CAD. Diametrically opposed to India's interest.
ACTION: For India ops: watch for crude-linked cost compression in manufacturing and logistics — opportunity to lock in forward contracts at lower rates. For Canada ops: assess Alberta exposure and energy sector positions. Don't buy TSX energy dips until Hormuz status is definitively resolved — it won't be. The "nuclear deterrent" framing from Fortune is correct — model Hormuz disruptions as a recurring cost from now on, not a one-off.
💭 Bear case: A genuine US-Iran nuclear deal unfreezes ~$20B for Iran and leads to full Hormuz opening — this would crash WTI toward $65-70, devastating Canadian oil sands economics but creating a massive windfall for India. The probability is higher than markets currently price.

AI · Future of Work ↑ Now – 5 years WATCH
AI isn't just automating tasks — it's separating expertise from the body that holds it. From Chinese film directors to factory floors, a knowledge enclosure is underway.

The Ken's Zero Shot newsletter — one of the sharpest AI reads on the internet — frames the current AI moment around "knowledge enclosure": expertise is being extracted from human bodies, encoded, and made reproducible at scale. Zero Shot's live event asked: which Indian companies will survive agentic AI? The stress test focused on Cred — suggesting that loyalty/rewards businesses built on data moats are highly vulnerable when AI agents can negotiate better deals autonomously.

SO WHAT: The companies most exposed to knowledge enclosure are those whose moat IS the expertise of their people — consulting firms, law firms, specialty service businesses. The companies least exposed are those with physical assets, regulatory licenses, distribution networks, or brand relationships that can't be digitized. India angle: India's IT outsourcing industry — which monetizes human expertise at scale — faces its most existential challenge. Canada angle: Bay Street legal and consulting firms face the same enclosure threat. But Canadian natural resources, infrastructure, and permitting expertise — which requires physical, jurisdictional, political knowledge — is significantly more enclosure-resistant.
ACTION: Map your business portfolio against the enclosure exposure spectrum. Pure knowledge businesses (consulting, legal, outsourced services) need an urgent AI strategy. Businesses with physical, regulatory, or relational moats have 2–5 years of natural protection — use them to build the AI layer before the enclosure catches up.
💭 The "knowledge enclosure" framing overestimates AI's ability to replicate tacit, contextual, trust-based expertise. A film director's aesthetic sensibility can be replicated visually — but the relationships, the funding dynamics, the festival strategy that makes a film career cannot. Expertise markets will bifurcate, not collapse.

🔭 The Long Game
Watch what happens to India's IT outsourcing sector over the next 18 months. The confluence of three forces — AI knowledge enclosure (The Ken/Zero Shot), Meta-style agentic workforce restructuring, and the organizational singularity thesis (1 person + AI outperforming 100 without it) — creates a pressure gradient that India's $250B IT export sector has never experienced before. The sector has survived Y2K panic, dot-com, GFC, and offshoring booms and busts. But those were demand shocks. This is a supply-side disruption: AI is producing the output the sector sells at dramatically lower cost. Companies like TCS, Infosys, and Wipro that can transition from selling person-hours to selling managed AI agents with human oversight may survive and thrive — they have the client relationships, the security clearances, and the global delivery infrastructure. Those that can't will be commoditized faster than any previous cycle. The parallel in Canada: consulting and professional services firms on Bay Street face the same enclosure. The Canadian firms with physical, regulatory, and resource-extraction expertise — mining law, energy permitting, infrastructure finance — have a natural moat that knowledge enclosure cannot yet cross. That moat has a 5-year clock, not a 20-year one.
