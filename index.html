The Hormuz Yo-Yo

Sunday, April 19, 2026 · Written 18:00 ET · For leosuhail@gmail.com
Iran learned the price of closing the Strait. Now it's operating the toll booth.

Editor's Read — 90 Seconds

Two days after Friday's risk-on orgy — S&P, Nasdaq, Bitcoin all at ATHs, crude down 11% on the week — Iran re-closed the Strait of Hormuz on Saturday night. Not the full 72-hour blockade of April 14-17, just a 14-hour "shipping inspection" exercise that locked 22 VLCCs before a deal was cut at 4am Sunday. Crude is back at $92 Brent, $88 WTI in Asian overnight trading. Nifty futures in Singapore down 1.8%.

The signal is not the closure. The signal is that Iran has discovered a revenue-neutral, geopolitically-priced option: it can open and close the Strait at 10-day intervals, extracting concessions each cycle without triggering the military response a sustained blockade would. The April 21 ceasefire deadline is now structurally irrelevant — Iran doesn't want peace, it wants a permanent toll booth.

Meanwhile Carney's Sunday-afternoon federal fuel excise suspension (April 20–Sept 7, 10¢/L gasoline, 4¢/L diesel) is the most decisive Canadian fiscal intervention since the 2020 wage subsidy. It prices the Hormuz Yo-Yo into domestic policy — Ottawa has implicitly conceded oil stays elevated for 5 months. India's Finance Ministry is signaling a parallel excise cut by May 10. The "Hormuz dividend" narrative from Friday is dead before the ink dried.

Under all of this: the Anthropic "Mythos" federal-government AI memo leaked to Axios on Saturday, Capital One's AI-agent customer-service pivot, and a quiet Crunchbase Q1 data release showing global VC up 47% YoY. These would be the lead in a normal week. This is not a normal week.

Signal Spotlight

The One Thing That Matters

Hormuz is no longer a crisis. It is a cycle.

Markets priced Friday's reopening as a durable peace. Saturday's re-closure proved that assumption is now permanently wrong. Iran has learned — through two live-fire experiments in one week — that it can close-open-close the Strait on approximately 10-day intervals and extract concessions without crossing the military-response threshold. This changes everything downstream of oil: central bank paths, Canadian-Indian fiscal posture, energy equities, airlines, EM FX, EV adoption curves.

The correct trade is not "long oil" or "short oil." The correct trade is long volatility, short certainty. Anyone pricing a terminal Hormuz outcome in either direction is the counterparty.

Why the consensus is wrongEvery Wall Street note this weekend is framing Saturday as "one-off." The pattern is two closures in seven days. Base rate says this is now a regime, not an incident. Watch for the next closure between April 26–30.

The Lead

Geopolitics · Energy · Shipping BREAKING · SAT NIGHT

Iran Re-Closes Hormuz For 14 Hours, Then Walks Away With Concessions

At 19:40 local time Saturday, Iranian Revolutionary Guard fast-boats launched a coordinated "shipping inspection" sweep across three lanes of the Strait of Hormuz, diverting 22 VLCCs and 8 LPG carriers into pre-declared waiting zones off Bandar Abbas. The stated pretext: "flag verification under IMO 2026 tonnage rules." The practical effect: a 14-hour hard-stop on 16 million barrels per day of seaborne crude flow.

By 09:40 Sunday, Tehran announced vessels could transit "pending bilateral review" — code for the quiet deal cut with Omani intermediaries overnight. Our read on the concessions package: softened sanctions enforcement on 4 Iranian-flagged tankers, a USD 1.1B SWIFT unfreeze for oil-revenue receivables, and a tacit understanding that the April 21 ceasefire window will be "re-scheduled" (not extended, not cancelled — a calculated ambiguity).

The market response: Brent leapt from $92.10 to $96.40 within three hours of the closure announcement, then back to $92.80 by Sunday afternoon as the deal emerged. Net WoW: crude still down ~7% from Friday's open but now decisively off the Hormuz-peace lows. The $80 WTI handle is dead until further notice.

So WhatIran has discovered it can operate the Strait as a revenue-neutral concessions machine. Close for 12–24 hours, extract a bilateral deal, re-open. Repeat every ~10 days. This is a fundamentally different regime than "crisis → war → peace." It is "infrastructure → toll → loop."

The Canadian File 🍁

Fiscal · Ottawa CarneySUNDAY

Carney Suspends Federal Fuel Excise Tax April 20 – September 7

In a rare Sunday-afternoon press conference from Rideau Cottage, PM Mark Carney announced a five-month federal fuel excise suspension: 10¢/litre off gasoline, 4¢/litre off diesel, effective 00:01 Monday April 20 through midnight Sunday September 7. Estimated Treasury cost: CAD $3.8B. Explicit justification: "protecting Canadian households from the Iranian war tax."

This is the most decisive Canadian fiscal move since the 2020 CEWS wage subsidy. It concedes, in a single stroke, that (a) oil stays elevated for at least 5 months, (b) the BoC cannot shoulder the inflation-fighting burden alone, and (c) the Liberals intend to own the affordability narrative into any October election calendar. Ottawa has just priced Hormuz Yo-Yo into federal policy.

So WhatCarney has set a template. India, UK, Germany, Australia will face political pressure to match. This is the first coordinated fiscal response to the new Hormuz regime.
ActionLong Canadian consumer discretionary (LNR, MG, CTC). Long TSX retailers. Fade short-end CAD rates — fiscal-over-monetary stance steepens the curve.

The Long Game

Three Structural Shifts To Hold In Your Head

1. Infrastructure as instrument. Iran's Hormuz Yo-Yo is a template. Expect similar cycles from other actors controlling chokepoints: Turkey at the Bosporus, Egypt at Suez, Panama. The next decade's geopolitics will be priced in 10-day increments.

2. Fiscal over monetary. Carney's fuel-tax move, India's forthcoming excise cut, and forthcoming European equivalents mark the transition from central-bank-led to treasury-led inflation management. Central banks become reactive, not leading. Yield curves steepen structurally.

3. The AI + government memo. Anthropic Mythos is the first of many. Every major AI lab will end up with a signature federal contract before IPO. This is the new "first oil contract" of the frontier-model era — and the valuation floor it sets is reshaping the entire private market above it.
