Today's gravitational center is the slow-motion war over the Federal Reserve's independence — and nobody is treating it with the seriousness it deserves. Trump threatened this week to fire Powell the moment his chairman term expires May 15. The Supreme Court is expected to rule any day in Trump v. Cook, a case that will determine whether a president can fire a Fed governor. If the Court says yes, the institutional architecture that has underpinned global monetary policy since Bretton Woods begins to crack. This is not hyperbole. It is the single most consequential pending legal ruling for markets in years.

Here's the cross-reference I'm not seeing enough of: The same week Trump attacks Fed independence, we learn from The Ken that India's manufacturing boom is almost entirely structurally dependent on China — assembly happening in India, design and IP staying in Beijing. Dixon Technologies earns 2% margins, runs on PLI subsidies, and if those dry up has no standalone viability. Apple vendors exported $2.5B in components not to the US, but back to China. Indian EV makers face rare-earth magnet shortages that could halt production by mid-July. The thesis that India is "decoupling" from China is, I think, almost entirely false. India is becoming more embedded in China's supply chain, not less — just one layer removed.

Now connect those two signals: A hawkish Fed (or an unpredictable post-Powell Fed) keeps USD strong. A strong dollar keeps INR and CAD weak simultaneously — India's import costs rise, making Chinese components cheaper relative to domestic alternatives, deepening the dependency. Canada faces the same dollar trap: BRP just got hit with $500M in US tariff costs and pulled its 2027 guidance. Both countries are price-takers to a US/China axis that is pulling in opposite directions.

My call: The Supreme Court exempts the Fed from presidential firing power this week. Trump loses legally, doubles down rhetorically, Warsh gets confirmed with Tillis opposition dissolved after the probe wraps. Markets get a one-day relief rally. Then reality sets back in: a 4.3% Fed funds rate into a slowing global economy, with both India and Canada squeezed from both sides.

The Fed's Independence Is on Trial — and Trump Isn't Waiting for the Verdict

Trump told Fox Business he will fire Jerome Powell if he doesn't leave his Fed governor seat after his chairman term expires May 15. Powell has said explicitly he has "no intention" of leaving until the DOJ's probe of the Fed's $2.5B HQ renovation ends. The standoff is now three-sided: Trump vs. Powell, Trump vs. Sen. Thom Tillis (R-NC) blocking Kevin Warsh's nomination until the probe concludes, and the White House vs. the Supreme Court, which is expected to rule imminently on Trump v. Cook — whether the president can fire a Fed governor for any reason. Oral arguments suggested the Court is skeptical of Trump's position.

SO WHAT — India: A politicized Fed = hawkish rates by proxy (markets will demand a risk premium for Fed credibility loss). INR faces double pressure: existing CA deficit + potential Dollar surge. For India's RBI, a US precedent of political monetary policy removes the last moral pressure to keep central banking independent. SO WHAT — Canada: GoC 10Y is already falling toward 3.4% as Iran ceasefire progress eases energy shock. But if Fed independence erodes and US rates become politically unpredictable, BoC faces impossible cross-pressure: cut to address tariff-driven growth slowdown, or hold to maintain CAD stability relative to a volatile Fed. That's the bind.
ACTION: Watch Supreme Court ruling — expected any day. If Court protects Fed independence, expect a brief rates + equity rally. If Court sides with Trump, sell duration and watch gold re-test $5,000. For Canadian portfolios: long GoC bonds if BoC rate cuts accelerate. For India: monitor RBI's real rate buffer — currently thin.
💭 Bear case: The Court protects the Fed, Trump backs down, Warsh gets confirmed as a nominal hawk who actually cuts rates faster than Powell would have — making Trump's whole campaign counterproductive but markets not caring.

The legal architecture: Under the Federal Reserve Act, governors can only be removed "for cause" — meaning malfeasance or neglect of duty, not policy disagreement. The DOJ probe of the Fed's renovation is Trump's attempt to manufacture "cause." Even if Warsh is confirmed as chair, Powell as governor would still have a vote on the FOMC. The real prize Trump wants is to control all seven governor votes, not just the chairmanship. The irony: if Trump succeeds in installing a politically loyal Fed board, the resulting inflation (from rate cuts ordered for political reasons) would likely hurt his base most — exactly the people who elected him on anti-inflation grounds.

Historical context: Every time a president has tried to control the Fed — Nixon pressuring Arthur Burns to cut rates pre-1972 election, Reagan clashing with Volcker — the long-term outcome has been inflation or a credibility crisis. The bond market is the ultimate enforcer. With $35T in US debt outstanding, bond vigilantes have more firepower than any White House.

SO WHAT: The "Make in India" narrative is primarily an assembly story, not a manufacturing sovereignty story. The domestic value addition is under 20%; China's is ~40%. This is structurally dangerous for anyone doing business in India: supply chain risk is not reduced, it's obfuscated. The disruption arrives suddenly — a Chinese export restriction triggers a cascade of production halts across electronics, pharma, and EV sectors simultaneously. The magnet story is the canary. ACTION — India: Evaluate supply chain exposure in any India manufacturing investment. Companies with true domestic IP (like Agnikul Cosmos in space, or specialty chemical firms) are worth premium. 💭 Optimistic counter: India has gone from near-zero to 8% of global smartphone assembly in 4 years — that IS real progress. The PLI cliff is real but manageable if India upgrades R&D spend from 0.6% to even 1.5% of revenue over five years.

The Dubai routing: More than 2,300 Indian companies now operate in Jebel Ali Free Zone, using Dubai as a staging ground for global trade with fewer geopolitical frictions. This is globalisation with detours — India is routing supply chains through intermediaries rather than building independent capability. The same pattern plays out in pharma: when Chinese API producers cut prices 40-50%, Indian domestic producers sold below cost. INDIA · POLICY ↑ Active Now ACT
India's IBC 2.0 Just Became Law — Creditors Win, Debtors Beware

The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code Amendment Act 2026 received the President's assent last week. Key changes: the lookback period for fraudulent transactions extended to two years (previously ambiguous, assumed ~1 year); a new CIIRP (Creditor Initiated Insolvency Resolution Process) allows banks to resolve stressed assets pre-NCLT, faster and cheaper; government dues no longer jump the secured creditor queue (overturning the Supreme Court's Rainbow Papers ruling); malicious filings now carry fines of ₹1 lakh to ₹2 crore. Timeline violations that stretched resolution from 330 to 600+ days are directly addressed. Over ₹3.5 lakh crore of distressed debt has been resolved under IBC since 2016.

SO WHAT — India: This is creditor-friendly legislation at a time when India needs foreign capital inflows. Banks and NBFCs get significantly more power to act on stressed assets. For startups: this changes the math on secured debt — if you default, lenders move faster. For investors: it improves the risk-adjusted return on Indian private credit. The CIIRP mechanism is particularly important for mid-market restructurings where full NCLT proceedings are prohibitive. SO WHAT — Canada: Canadian institutional investors (pensions, banks) with India exposure should run through their portfolio covenants under the new lookback rules — any related-party transactions in the last 2 years on stressed Indian assets are now scrutinizable.
ACTION: If you hold distressed Indian debt or are considering it: the IBC 2.0 dramatically improves recovery timelines. Run CIIRP eligibility analysis on any stressed credits in your India portfolio. For corporate borrowers in India: document all major related-party transactions now — the 2-year lookback window is active from the date of first petition filing, not admission.
💭 The implementation risk is real: IBC 2016 looked great on paper too. NCLT tribunals remain under-resourced, resolution professionals vary wildly in quality, and the CIIRP mechanism is untested. New law, old institutional bottlenecks.
Read original → Gmail source

The CIIRP is the most consequential new mechanism. Think of it as a pre-pack insolvency process: banks can start a structured resolution before the company fully collapses into full CIRP, keeping management in place under supervision, at lower cost. This is directionally what the UK Scheme of Arrangement or US Chapter 11 pre-pack does. If implemented well, it could reduce the "zombie company" problem in India — firms technically solvent but bleeding cash — by enabling faster, quieter restructuring. The government dues issue (overturning Rainbow Papers) is significant for PE/VC investors in India: tax-claim priority ambiguity was a meaningful deterrent to secondary buyouts of stressed companies.

Elon's Terafab Is Real: Musk Is Willing to Pay Well Above Market to Build a US Chip Factory

Elon Musk's Terafab team has contacted chip industry suppliers for price quotes and delivery times for chipmaking equipment, offering to pay "considerably above" quoted figures in exchange for priority. Terafab's strategy relies on raw scale and vertical integration — bypassing the ASML EUV lithography bottleneck (the machine that makes advanced chips, of which ASML is effectively the global monopolist) by using alternative exposure techniques and sheer manufacturing volume. This is the semiconductor sovereignty play that the US government has wanted but no private actor has tried at Musk-scale.

SO WHAT — India: India's semiconductor ambitions (Tata's fab partnership, ISMC project) are smaller-scale versions of this exact play. If Terafab succeeds in demonstrating that you can build advanced chips outside the ASML monopoly, it opens a pathway for India's fab program that currently looks nearly impossible given ASML export controls and technology dependency. Watch for India-Terafab technology licensing discussions in 12-24 months. SO WHAT — Canada: Canada's rare minerals (lithium, cobalt, rare earths) are inputs into chip manufacturing. A domestic US chip fab running at Terafab's scale increases demand for North American critical mineral supply — directly good for Canadian mining sector.
ACTION: Monitor Canadian critical mineral companies with US chip fab supply relationships. If Terafab accelerates, demand for ultra-pure chemicals, specialty gases, and rare earth materials made in North America jumps significantly. For India: track whether Tata's semiconductor roadmap shifts to engage with Terafab as a technology or market partner rather than competing alone.
💭 Semiconductors are where "move fast and break things" meets physics. ASML's EUV monopoly exists because the technology took 30 years and $10 billion+ to develop. Musk's DOGE-style "light speed" timeline expectations may be fantasy when applied to nanometer-scale precision manufacturing.
Read original → Gmail source

The ASML bottleneck: ASML's extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines are the only way to print the most advanced chip patterns. One machine costs ~$350M, takes a year to deliver, and ASML produces roughly 60 per year. The US has restricted ASML from selling to China. Terafab's bypass strategy likely involves Deep Ultraviolet (DUV) lithography with multi-patterning — less efficient but ASML-independent. This is exactly what China's SMIC has been forced to do under US sanctions. The irony: Terafab may be learning from China's sanctions-forced semiconductor independence journey.

The embodied AI thesis: Yann LeCun and Fei-Fei Li both argue that LLMs have hit a scaling ceiling and that world models — AI that understands physical space the way animals navigate environments — are the path to AGI. Humanoid robots are the training environment for world models. Each deployed robot generates physical-world data that improves the next generation. This is why Nvidia is so interested: it sells the compute for both training and inference, and physical AI multiplies the required compute by orders of magnitude. The "everything machine" thesis means the humanoid robot market isn't just manufacturing — it's every environment humans currently navigate.
