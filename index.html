Three things are converging in the next 36 hours and the market is pricing none of them correctly. First: Tim Cook is out at Apple — reports overnight confirm John Ternus, the hardware engineering chief, is the heir apparent, with a formal announcement possibly as early as this week. Second: Anthropic and AWS just closed a $25B compute expansion with a 5GW Project Rainier commitment on a million Trainium2 chips by year-end. Third: the US–Iran ceasefire expires at midnight tomorrow, and Trump told reporters overnight that an extension is "highly unlikely." Any one of these would dominate a normal week. They are landing in the same 48-hour window.

The cross-currents matter more than any single headline. Apple's succession signals the end of the services-first era and a return to hardware-defined ambition — which directly benefits the AI-PC and custom-silicon trade (AMD, ARM, the Trainium story). Amazon's $25B commits public-cloud AI compute to one lab at a scale that makes OpenAI's Microsoft-only posture a strategic liability. And the ceasefire clock means the third Hormuz event (P150-1) is not a "could happen" — it's a scheduling question.

The India read: CPI risk is now a fuel-price problem, not a food-price problem. WTI closed at $88.60 Monday after the Touska fallout. If Iran breaks cleanly on Wednesday, $100 is back on the table by early May. RBI has no room. Every Indian OMC and every import-exposed D2C brand is now a beta on one number I can't control. Position for this before the ceasefire expires, not after.

The Canada read is sharper: Canadian CPI came in at 2.4% for March with gasoline +21.2% year-on-year — the largest single-month energy surge on record. The Globe framed this as "BoC inflation surprise." It is not a surprise. It is the Hormuz Yo-Yo landing in Canadian grocery receipts. BoC meets April 29. Any remaining path to a cut is gone. And Carney's April 19 excise cut was prescient — he is 10 days ahead of the data.

The call I'm making today: the next 36 hours re-price three tail risks simultaneously. Use them.

The Long Game
The week's convergent events all point one direction: compute is the new petroleum. Apple's succession to a hardware CEO, Anthropic's 5GW commitment, Meta's 8,000-person reallocation into AI pods, and India/Canada's desperate scramble for sovereign compute announcements — these are pieces of the same picture. The strategic geography of the next decade is not about oil lanes or semiconductor fabs alone. It is about gigawatt pipelines to inference clusters, measured in grid connection points and substation capacity rather than refinery throughput. Every nation that does not lock in 10GW of dedicated AI compute by 2028 becomes a capacity consumer, not a capacity producer. Canada has roughly 18 months to make a real commitment; India has roughly the same, with a harder power problem. The sovereign move is not to fund a "national champion" — it is to secure the electrons. Watch the Canadian throne speech in May, and India's Union Budget in February 2027.
