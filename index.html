This is what I warned about on April 19: Iran learned that the strait works "almost like a nuclear deterrent," and now the US is calling the bluff. We are no longer watching a diplomatic pause. We are watching round two.

The most interesting market response this morning isn't in oil — it's in equities that didn't break. Nifty opened flat at 24,338, barely below Friday's close. The S&P futures are pricing a marginal red open, not a gap-down. The VIX is at 18.20, not 25. Translation: the tape believes the US can enforce the blockade without losing control. That is a stronger claim than the ceasefire-rally trade. It means the market is now long-USN, short-Tehran — a thesis that will test very quickly if Iran retaliates through a Houthi or IRGC proxy this week. My P151-1 call is below: I'm flagging 65% probability the ceasefire is formally renounced by either side before April 30. If that hits, oil goes $95+ and the India tailwind I kept flagging inverts violently.

The India-Canada split is now the sharpest I've seen it all year. India imports 87% of its crude — the +$5/bbl move since Friday wipes out roughly $6B of annual savings. The rupee opened soft at ₹93.18/USD. At the same time, Alberta Premier Smith is reportedly preparing an emergency budget revision upward because Brent-linked royalty revenues just jumped an estimated $2.1B annualized. Two countries in my book, same barrel of oil, opposite fiscal consequences. This is the week to re-check portfolio hedges on both sides: India refiners should see margin compression Monday (BPCL, HPCL in focus), while Canadian oil sands names (CNQ, Suncor) are set to gap up 3-5% at the TSX open. Don't chase either move — wait for the retaliation/no-retaliation signal Tehran sends in the next 48 hours.

The quieter story I want you to hold onto: Cohere announced it is consolidating its global HQ in Toronto and committing $4.2B in Canadian R&D over five years. Carney's government simultaneously announced an $890M Sovereign Compute Fund. This is Canada belatedly doing what India did with the Semicon Mission and what the US did with the CHIPS Act. For the first time since I started publishing this brief, Canada is competing on AI industrial policy rather than hoping its talent doesn't leave. It won't change the Touska headlines this week. It may change the investment map for the next decade.

Geopolitics · Oil ⚡ Immediate + structural ACT
USS Spruance seizes Iranian tanker Touska in the Gulf of Oman. Tehran calls it an act of war. The ceasefire is effectively dead and WTI just gapped to $89.

At 03:14 GMT Sunday, the guided-missile destroyer USS Spruance, operating under a US Fifth Fleet sanctions-enforcement warrant, intercepted and boarded the VLCC Touska roughly 40 nm southeast of Muscat. The tanker was reportedly carrying ~2M barrels of Iranian-origin crude masked via ship-to-ship transfer off Sohar. Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi said the seizure is "a direct act of war" and that "the so-called ceasefire has been broken by Washington, not by Iran." IRGC Navy commander Tangsiri issued a separate statement promising "response at a time and place of our choosing." Pentagon spokesperson framed the boarding as "routine enforcement of a UN-sanctioned cargo." This is the first US seizure of an Iranian-flagged vessel since 2023.

The market move was immediate: WTI opened $89.10 in Singapore (+$5.25/bbl, +6.26%), Brent $92.80, and Singapore 0.5% fuel oil cracks widened 40 cents. The $118 spike high from the March crisis is now the obvious upside target if Iran retaliates against a single Saudi, UAE, or Kuwaiti-flagged vessel in response. Insurance rates on the Hormuz route, which had been easing back to ~$125k/voyage, are now being re-quoted at $250k+ per Bloomberg shipping desk.

SO WHAT IN INDIA: India imports 87% of its crude and the bulk transits Hormuz. A sustained $5/bbl move erodes ~$6B of annual import savings and directly pressures the CAD. Watch BPCL, HPCL, IOC for margin compression at open — OMCs will be forced to absorb near-term input cost rises before any retail pass-through. INR at ₹93.18/USD is the weakest print since December. SO WHAT IN CANADA: Canadian oil producers get a fiscal windfall. Alberta heavy oil differentials are already tightening. TSX energy is set for +3-5% at the open — CNQ, Suncor, Cenovus, MEG Energy are the obvious beneficiaries. Alberta 2026 budget will likely be revised up $2B+.

E-Comm · Tariffs ↑ 3–6 quarters ACT
Amazon launches "Great Wall Domestic" (GWD) fulfillment for Chinese merchants. Every Shein/Temu seller now has a tariff-cleared Amazon path into the US market.

Announced Saturday via the AWS China blog and quietly confirmed by Andy Jassy on an internal call leaked to The Information: Amazon has opened a bonded warehouse network in Mexico (Queretaro, Monterrey) and the Dominican Republic (Santo Domingo) specifically for Chinese merchants. Sellers ship in bulk to these nodes, Amazon takes "importer of record" status for the US border crossing, assumes the tariff liability at its pre-negotiated rate, and handles final-mile via the existing AMZN logistics network. Sellers pay a flat $3.80/unit fee plus 8% of landed value. No more de minimis dependency, no more customs surprise. Fully compliant with the 2026 tariff regime.

SO WHAT: Amazon just built the only legal Temu. For Chinese sellers who had been priced out of US distribution after the de minimis loophole closed, GWD is the reopening of the market. For Shein and Temu, this is a direct competitive threat — Amazon's existing 200M Prime subscribers have a higher ARPU than anything Temu can assemble. For AMZN, this adds a high-margin B2B services line that doesn't cannibalize 1P retail.
ACTION: AMZN is the cleanest tariff-proof play in large-cap US. Add on any pullback. Short PDD (Temu parent) on any rally — the moat just leaked. India parallel: Flipkart/Meesho will need a version of this within 18 months or cede cross-border to AMZN.
💭 The program depends on Amazon maintaining its pre-negotiated tariff rate with USTR. If that rate is renegotiated upward, margins compress hard.
