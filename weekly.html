3 THE BIGGEST MISS

We underweighted the speed of Hormuz escalation and its macro consequences.

The Brief flagged the Hormuz risk all month, but the blockade's *timing* and *simultaneity* with Hungary's political rupture caught the newsletter flat-footed. We positioned it as a geopolitical story, not a macro story. The real miss: we didn't forecast that oil above $85/bbl would immediately trigger FPI selling in India. The repricing happened in 24 hours, not over weeks. Capital flows were faster and more coordinated than expected.

What we should have done: Layered in India earnings revisions *before* the blockade began, so that when oil spiked, we had already flagged the earnings hit. Instead, we lagged the market by a day. For next week, we'll run earnings forecasts *simultaneously* with macro events, not sequentially.

6 ONE BIG IDEA
The Control Layer: Why AI Agents Are Replacing SaaS

This week exposed a profound shift in how capital flows through technology infrastructure. It's not incremental—it's structural. And it starts with a simple recognition: autonomous agents have a cost of failure that legacy software never did.

When a Figma designer loses an hour to a UI bug, the company absorbs the cost. When an autonomous agent makes a wrong decision about inventory, procurement, or customer communications, the company loses revenue, reputation, or customer trust. The cost of error has multiplied 100-fold. This is why agent safety—the governance layer that supervises, audits, and rolls back autonomous decisions—isn't a feature. It's the entire product category that will define the next decade of enterprise software.

Guild AI, Sycamore, and a dozen stealth startups are racing to own this layer. They're not building AI better. They're building the *guardrails* for AI. And capital is flowing there at jaw-dropping speeds because enterprise IT leaders have exactly one job: reduce the blast radius of AI mistakes. The SaaS companies that thrived in the 2010s solved *process automation*. The companies that win in 2026-2030 will solve *autonomous decision governance*. Figma can't compete because it's not a governance platform—it's a tool. Tools are fungible. Governance layers are defensible.

Meanwhile, Anthropic's $30B run rate isn't about being the "best" AI model. It's about scale. 1,000+ enterprise customers spending $1M+/year means Anthropic has achieved something OpenAI is still chasing: multi-year contracts that fund inference at volume. That's not a software business. That's infrastructure. And infrastructure businesses win by controlling critical paths, not by innovating faster. Anthropic's Claude now runs 80% of enterprise inference workloads in a world where nobody is confident enough to run unmanaged agents. The $30B rate is the floor, not the ceiling.

The real question for next week: Will earnings season reveal that CIOs have already committed budget to agent safety infrastructure? If so, SaaS compression accelerates. If not, there's still time to rotate back. But the signals from this week—Anthropic's scaling, Guild's emergence, Nvidia backing RISC-V for open-source silicon control—all point in one direction: governance infrastructure is eating the enterprise software world. And unlike SaaS, it's defensible.
