The Narrative

March opened with lingering recession anxiety from February's mini-crash, but sentiment shifted abruptly in the second week. The Fed's hawkish forward guidance—signaling zero rate cuts in 2026—landed differently than expected: relief that monetary policy had stabilized, not shock at intransigence. Employment data beat hard (new jobless claims fell, payroll growth accelerated), signaling the jobs market wasn't fractured by rising rates after all. The Brief's call that "the recession trade was priced for catastrophe" proved prescient; equities bounced 2–3% on those prints.

But the real gravitational shift came from what happened in AI. NVIDIA's GTC (March 16–19) marked a pivotal inflection: the industry moved from "will agentic AI be real?" to "how do we deploy it?" Google's March announcements of Gemini expansions across Workspace, Search Live, and Maps showed that the agentic paradigm isn't a research thesis anymore—it's shipping to hundreds of millions of users. OpenAI's reasoning models (o3, GPT-5.4 "Thinking") demonstrated that AI systems can now tackle economically meaningful tasks at or above human expert level. This wasn't a market surprise; it was a regime confirmation.

March's second half soured. US-Iran tensions escalated (though fell short of direct armed conflict), reintroducing geopolitical risk premium into equities. Credit markets, which had stabilized in early March, began flashing yellow lights: high-yield spreads widened 35 basis points, and the Treasury curve flattened further. Private credit concerns—the Brief warned about systemic risks in the shadow banking complex—started to unsettle institutional portfolios. Equities fell 2–3% in the final week, with tech and financials hit hardest.

Month-end picture: A market still processing the reality of AI's acceleration but now questioning the path forward under rising geopolitical risk. Equities down 5%, but crypto up 12%. Bonds volatile but holding. The divide between "AI will matter" and "but at what macro cost?" is the real story. And it's not resolved.

Letter From the Editor

Suhail Seth | March 31, 2026

The March market was a collision between two futures. One—driven by AI's acceleration into real deployment—points upward: new capabilities unlocking new value. The other—driven by geopolitical risk and credit stress—points sideways or down: uncertainty demanding hedges, deleveraging, caution. Both are real. Both are pricing into assets right now. The question is which one wins over the next six months.

Let me be direct: The AI story is overstated in one way and understated in another. Overstated because everyone knows about it. The NVIDIA stock price, the Google announcements, the ChatGPT-to-GPT-5 arc—these are mainstream now. No edge there. But understated in its implications for capital allocation and economic structure. The deployment of agentic AI means that the systems building, deploying, and maintaining AI infrastructure will see capex flow like never before. That's Nvidia (chips), Broadcom (networking), ASML (fab equipment), tsmc (fabrication), and a dozen supply chain players most retail investors don't follow. That capex acceleration is not priced in because most investors are still thinking about "AI stocks" as generative AI consumer apps. Wrong frame.

The geopolitical risk is murkier. US-Iran tensions are real, but so far they've remained at the posturing level. Markets priced a 15–20% tail risk of escalation into actual conflict; that put a 2–3% bid into equities in March's final week, especially defensive sectors. If tensions cool, that bid reverses, and we rally. If they escalate, we'll see another 3–5% drawdown and a move into duration and gold. But here's the asymmetry: AI capex is bottom-up and real. Geopolitical escalation is top-down and uncertain. Over a 12-month horizon, I'm betting on capex to dominate.

The divide between "AI will matter" and "but at what macro cost?" is the real story. It's not resolved.

The credit concern is the wildcard. Private credit has become a material pocket of leverage in the financial system—pension funds, insurance companies, and endowments all deployed capital into what they thought was "illiquid but safe" strategies. March's HY spread widening suggests investors are starting to re-evaluate that thesis. If private credit really does crack—if realized defaults spike or mark-to-market losses show up—then we'll see a liquidity crisis and forced deleveraging across all assets. But that's a tail scenario, not base case. Still, it's why I'm watching credit spreads like a hawk this April.

Here's my take for the month ahead: We're in a "two speed" market. Growth names tied to AI capex (foundries, chipmakers, cloud infrastructure) will outperform. Defensive and value will underperform because the geopolitical risk that was supporting them is fading slightly (no major escalation in late March). Crypto continues higher because it's both an inflation hedge and a macro liquidity indicator—when money is loose, it rallies; when it's tight, it crashes. We'll get another test in April earnings; if tech guides up on AI spending, we rally hard. If guidance is cautious, we consolidate.

The bottom line: March proved that AI is real, recession risk is lower, and geopolitical uncertainty is the price we all pay for the moment. For the next six months, I'm overweighting AI infrastructure (with hedges for geopolitical tail risk), neutral on financials (credit spreads are the tell), and neutral on energy (demand uncertainty trumps supply concerns). Crypto gets a small core position because the macro setup—ongoing liquidity, structural uncertainty, institutional adoption—favors it.

The market is not giving us a clear signal in either direction right now. That's fine. Markets don't always have to declare a direction. Our job is to position for what's real (AI capex, institutional crypto adoption) and hedge for what's possible (geopolitical escalation, credit dislocation). March did both. April will tell us if we got it right.
