The Strait of Hormuz is open again, and markets are throwing a party — S&P at its third consecutive all-time high, oil down 11%, BTC ripping to $78k. I should feel relieved. Instead, I keep thinking about the three Bloomberg headlines that landed in my inbox this week simultaneously: Anthropic's Mythos is too dangerous to release. The Bank of Canada held an emergency meeting with the Big Six banks about Mythos. Indian fintechs are nervously lobbying Anthropic for early access to Mythos. Three different geographies, one model, one week — and this model hasn't even shipped yet.

Here's the thread I can't stop pulling on: we just watched the world's biggest energy chokepoint close, paralyze European aviation, threaten to spike inflation globally, and then reopen — all in roughly three weeks. Simultaneously, a single AI model proved it could orchestrate the digital equivalent of a bank heist faster than any human hacker. The macro shock from Hormuz is resolving. The AI shock from Mythos is just beginning. And in both cases, Canada and India are sitting in analogous positions: consequential enough to feel the full pain (Canada's oil patch took it from both sides — energy windfall then crash; India's refiners buying discounted Iranian crude now face supply uncertainty again), but not at the table where the decisions are made.

What nobody is connecting: Milk Road confirmed Goldman Sachs, Charles Schwab, and Morgan Stanley all filed crypto moves in the same five-day window — $17.6 trillion in client assets building pipes to BTC. The Hormuz resolution is the proximate trigger for today's rally. But the structural driver for BTC's rise is the IMF's report that global public debt will hit 100% of world GDP by 2029. When governments can't raise rates without triggering fiscal crises, the central bank loses independence. BTC and gold aren't just risk-on plays anymore — they're fiscal collapse insurance. Both India and Canada are watching their currencies soften against the dollar (USD/INR ~84.20, CAD/USD at 0.725) for the same underlying reason: dollar dominance in a debt-bomb world.

My forward call: The companies that get Glasswing Project access to Mythos in the next 90 days will have an 18-month head start that no amount of money can easily buy back. This is the moment to be aggressive about lobbying Anthropic directly — whether you're a Canadian bank or an Indian fintech. The model will be released eventually. The question is whether you're battle-tested when it happens, or scrambling.

Hormuz Reopens: Oil Craters 11%, S&P Hits Third Straight Record, Airlines Soar

Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz "completely open" to commercial shipping on Friday, triggering the biggest single-day market move in months. WTI crude plunged 11.4% to $83.85 — still elevated but a dramatic relief from the $100+ territory feared just weeks ago. The S&P 500 closed at 7,126, its third consecutive all-time high, while the NYSE Airline Index surged more than 6% on hopes that jet fuel shortages threatening European aviation would ease. There are still significant caveats: Iran mandated ships use its approved route (which only a handful have used), Trump said the US blockade "will remain in full force" until a formal peace deal, and 135 million barrels of oil in stranded tankers still need to reach market. The IEA previously warned Europe had "maybe six weeks" of jet fuel left, and the head of the agency warned it could take "up to two years" to normalize global oil flows even with the strait open.

SO WHAT — India: India's refiners have been buying discounted Iranian crude throughout the crisis. Reopening reduces that discount arbitrage but eliminates supply uncertainty. A sustained oil price around $80-85 is neutral-to-positive for India's import bill and the rupee. Watch for the RBI to get more comfortable with its stance if crude stabilizes here. Canada: Canadian oil producers just experienced a windfall from $90+ crude — now prices retreat. Canadian energy stocks (which drove TSX underperformance Friday vs US indices) will feel near-term pain. But pipelines, LNG Canada, and oil sands producers built their business plans on $60-70 WTI — $84 is still very profitable. The real question for Canada is whether the US-Iran peace deal also reshapes US-Canada energy dynamics (Trump signed pipeline permits between Canada and the US this week — more below).
ACTION: Don't chase the "all-clear" rally blindly — Milk Road Macro got this right: "bull run or bull trap?" Geopolitics resolved partially. The debt bomb (IMF: 100% GDP by 2029) and the Fed's frozen rate posture haven't changed. If you have commodity exposure in Canadian energy, hold — $84 is still good. On Indian imports, the immediate pressure eases; the structural story of India securing diverse energy sources remains.
💭 The bear case: The ceasefire is 10 days. Iran's approved route is barely functional. Shipping experts say vessels remain wary. And Trump's blockade is still active. We may have priced in 90% of the good news from a situation that's maybe 40% resolved. Oil could snap back toward $90 on any diplomatic stumble, reversing all of Friday's gains.

Anthropic's Mythos Can Orchestrate Digital Bank Heists. Its Own Researchers Proved It.

Mythos can do this with zero human guidance after being given an objective. Anthropic responded by forming Project Glasswing, a restricted-access testing program where major tech companies and JPMorgan Chase stress-test Mythos against their own systems. The White House is separately preparing to give federal agencies access. Anthropic's CEO Dario Amodei met with White House chief of staff Susie Wiles this week despite Anthropic suing the Pentagon over "supply chain risk" designation — pragmatism over litigation when Mythos is on the table.

SO WHAT — India: Bloomberg confirmed Indian fintechs are nervously lobbying Anthropic for Mythos access — both for fear of being attacked by Mythos-powered hackers AND for wanting the productivity advantage. India's digital financial infrastructure (UPI, fintech stack, Aadhaar linkage) is a massive attack surface. If Mythos can find vulnerabilities faster than human teams can patch, every Indian bank and payments company is now in a race. Canada: Bank of Canada convened the Canadian Financial Sector Resiliency Group (Big Six banks, OSFI, Finance Dept, TMX Group) on April 10 specifically for Mythos. BoC Governor explicitly said this model shows the need to "come to grips" with AI risks. This is the most serious AI-related regulatory mobilization in Canadian financial history.
ACTION: Contact Anthropic about Glasswing Project access for any enterprise with significant digital infrastructure. This is not a "monitor and wait" situation — being in the stress-testing cohort gives you advance knowledge of your own vulnerabilities AND positions you as a trusted Anthropic partner for eventual Mythos commercial access. For India: push through industry bodies (NPCI, RBI fintech sandbox). For Canada: BoC/OSFI are the natural intermediaries — flag readiness to participate.

Anthropic's own Project Glasswing suggests controlled deployment is viable. The real risk isn't Mythos going rogue — it's the adversarial cat-and-mouse accelerating to a pace where human oversight becomes a bottleneck, not a safeguard.

IMF: Global Public Debt Will Hit 100% of World GDP by 2029. Welcome to Fiscal Dominance.

The IMF's latest Fiscal Monitor dropped a structural bomb that got buried under Hormuz headlines: global public debt is on track to reach 100% of world GDP by 2029, driven primarily by the US and China. Milk Road Macro's framing is precise: "We're borrowing like it's World War II, but without the post-war economic boom." The macro implication: when debt reaches this threshold, central banks lose independence because raising interest rates would trigger a fiscal crisis for their own government. The Fed's already frozen (the Beige Book this week confirmed a "no hire, no fire" economy where businesses won't make capital decisions until Middle East tensions resolve — and the odds of Fed rate cuts this year are near zero). Meanwhile, energy and metal input costs are rising faster than companies can raise prices, squeezing margins across all twelve Fed districts.

SO WHAT — India: India's own fiscal position is relatively better than G7 averages, but the global debt regime means the RBI faces structural constraints on rate policy independent of its domestic mandate. A fiscally dominant world means USD strength persists (flight to dollar liquidity), keeping USD/INR elevated and India's import costs structurally higher. Canada: CAD is a commodity-linked EM proxy currency — it benefits when oil rises and suffers when dollar strengthens. The fiscal dominance scenario (high debt → central banks can't raise rates → dollar remains strong → CAD structurally weak) is a slow-burn headwind for Canadian purchasing power internationally. Bank of Canada's hold at 2.25% reflects this bind.
ACTION: Hard assets (BTC, gold at $4,831) are not speculative in this environment — they're insurance. Review any long-dated fixed income exposure in both Indian and Canadian portfolios. The "risk-free" rate is becoming the "fiscal-risk" rate.
💭 100% debt-to-GDP is a political construct — Japan has been operating at 260% without fiscal collapse. What matters is whether the debt is denominated in local currency and whether there's domestic demand for it. The US has both advantages. The IMF's track record on crisis timing is historically poor.

The Ken: AI Is Creating a "Knowledge Enclosure." Expertise Is Being Separated from the Bodies That Hold It.

The Ken's Zero Shot column this week (Brady Ng) frames the most important long-term AI thesis with a striking image: filmmaker Jia Zhangke used Bytedance's Seedance 2.0 to create a film featuring two AI-generated versions of himself — one "with an AI feel," one "practically like the real me." The Ken's framing: "expertise is being separated from the body that holds it." From digital doubles to factory floors, AI is encoding expertise — not replacing workers in the traditional sense, but extracting, encoding, and making reproducible what previously required a specific person. The Zero Shot live event (180 attendees, April 12) stress-tested this against Indian companies. The panel asked: which Indian consumer internet companies survive agentic AI? CRED was live-tested. The implicit answer: companies whose moat is relationships and distribution (not knowledge work) survive best.

SO WHAT — India: This is the most important strategic lens for Indian business in 2026. India's comparative advantage has historically been knowledge-worker arbitrage — engineers, doctors, lawyers, accountants cheaper than Western equivalents. If AI extracts and commoditizes that expertise, the arbitrage disappears. The DPDP compliance wave (₹10,000 Cr market, see story below) is actually a countervailing force — it creates regulatory complexity that requires local knowledge that AI can't fully substitute. Canada: Same thesis applies to Canadian professional services — legal, accounting, consulting. The firms that survive are the ones whose value is relationships and judgment, not information retrieval.
ACTION: Audit which parts of your business depend on "expertise as competitive moat" vs. "relationships/distribution as competitive moat." The former is at risk faster than anyone's planning for. The latter is durable. Rebalance accordingly in both your Indian and Canadian operations.
💭 The "knowledge enclosure" frame assumes the AI encoding is accurate. But expertise is tacit, contextual, and often wrong in ways that experts know to flag. AI systems trained on recorded expertise will encode the best-articulated knowledge, not the deepest knowledge. The best experts will become more valuable, not less — as arbiters of what the AI gets wrong.

OpenAI's GPT-Rosalind: The Rise of Domain-Specific Frontier AI — Drug Discovery Gets Its Own Model

OpenAI launched GPT-Rosalind, named after Rosalind Franklin (who helped discover DNA's structure), the first frontier reasoning model built purely for life sciences. It has in-depth understanding of genomics, chemistry, and protein engineering; access to 50+ scientific databases; and scored higher than 95% of human experts at predicting how certain RNA sequences work in early tests. Access is restricted to approved organizations — Amgen, Moderna, Allen Institute, Thermo Fisher Scientific among first recipients. OpenAI's pitch: drug discovery takes 10-15 years from target to approval, with only 1 in 10 drugs making it. GPT-Rosalind attacks the bottleneck at the discovery and synthesis stage. The Deep View's editorial framing is right: this is part of a fundamental shift from one-model-to-rule-them-all to specialized, restricted frontier models for specific domains.

SO WHAT — India: India's pharma sector — the world's pharmacy, producing 20%+ of global generics — has a direct stake. Indian generics manufacturers and CROs (contract research organizations) could either be disrupted (if drug discovery gets faster and originators start producing their own generics via AI-assisted manufacturing) or empowered (if they gain GPT-Rosalind access for their own discovery pipelines). Sun Pharma, Dr. Reddy's, Cipla — watch whether they engage OpenAI for enterprise access. Canada: MaRS Discovery District, Montreal's AI-biotech cluster, and University of Toronto's medicine programs are exactly the "approved research" candidates OpenAI is vetting. Canada's academic-to-industry pipeline in life sciences could accelerate if institutional access comes through.
ACTION: If you have Indian pharma exposure, watch whether Cipla or Dr. Reddy's announces OpenAI partnerships — that would be a material signal. For Canadian life sciences investments, MaRS and U of T would be natural first-movers for GPT-Rosalind access.
💭 Restricted access models create regulatory capture — whoever gets early access shapes the model's use cases in ways that entrench their own research advantage. Big pharma using GPT-Rosalind first means the model optimizes for their patent-protected pathways, not generic drug discovery that would benefit India and the Global South most.

🔭 The Long Game
Slow-burn trend worth tracking

The "knowledge enclosure" The Ken describes — AI extracting expertise from human bodies and making it reproducible — will hit professional services first (legal, medical, accounting) and industrial expertise second (factory floor, engineering). But the third wave, which nobody is talking about yet, is governance expertise: the specialized knowledge of how to navigate regulators, build political relationships, and operate within specific national legal contexts. This is the last expertise that AI cannot replicate, because it requires presence, trust-building, and local social capital that no model can accumulate. For businesses operating across India and Canada simultaneously, the competitive moat of the next decade is not technology — it's the irreplaceable relationships with regulatory bodies on both sides. Anthropic getting Dario Amodei into the White House this week while simultaneously engaging BoC and Indian fintechs is showing exactly this playbook. The companies that survive the AI transition are the ones who used the window to build governance relationships while their competitors were busy benchmarking models.
