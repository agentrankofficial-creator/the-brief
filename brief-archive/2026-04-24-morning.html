Same Fed-dollar-oil vector hitting both my geographies; two completely different responses. India is absorbing. Canada is pricing the option. Position your capital accordingly — long Canadian infra/compute build-out, short Indian import-inflation beneficiaries until RBI steps in visibly.

SO WHAT: For India this is a direct P&L event. Canada is being paid to build optionality. For both: the Fed-dollar-oil vector is now the only macro that matters until May 15.

THE LONG GAME

The Hormuz-rupee-CUSMA cluster is not three stories. It is one story seen from three angles — and every story I will write in April and May will be a footnote to that observation. The Fed-dollar-oil-Iran triangle is the only macro operative through June, and my job in this brief is to resist the urge to make it feel new by giving it three names. It does not have three names. It has one: parallel-optionality versus absorption. Canada is building the option. India is absorbing the shock. Both are correct responses to the same input, indexed on different national-level timelines and tolerances.

The larger game is that for the first time since 2008, a single exogenous variable (Iran-Hormuz) is setting the pricing regime for the Fed, for the rupee, for the loonie, for US equities, and for global oil simultaneously. That is a correlation collapse. Diversification benefits compress when correlations converge, and the book that gets hit hardest is the "emerging-market-plus-commodity" balanced vehicle. For a private allocator — which is what I am — the answer is neither India nor Canada alone. It is a barbell: absorbing-side exposure in India at a discount (take advantage of the forced selling), and option-pricing exposure in Canada at a premium (pay up for the structural hedge). Six months from now, one of those two positions will have paid outsized. I do not know which. The edge is holding both.
