Today's gravitational center is a ship. The USS Spruance disabled the Touska with engine-room fire on Saturday, and that one act has resolved the ambiguity I described on Sunday: the Hormuz Yo-Yo is not a ceasefire accident — it is the regime. Iran now frames the ceasefire as breached. Trump's team flies to Islamabad for "round two" while simultaneously authorizing kinetic action. Wednesday's ceasefire expiry is no longer a deadline on paper; it is a trigger embedded inside an already-broken truce. WTI is up 6% in the overnight to $89. The market has stopped believing in a tidy peace.

But the story I want to plant a flag on is Canada. Mark Carney's Sunday video — "US ties are a weakness" — is not a speech, it is a thesis. Read it alongside three things that dropped the same week: (1) the $890M AI Sovereign Compute Infrastructure Program opening applications; (2) Cohere's public pledge to stay Canadian-headquartered as Aleph Alpha circles; (3) Canada's January trade pivot to China. What looks like reactive politics is in fact coherent industrial dirigisme. Ottawa is building sovereign compute, protecting sovereign champions, and diversifying sovereign markets — simultaneously.

Now layer this on top of Meta firing 8,000 people on May 20. Zuckerberg is not trimming cost. He is converting payroll into token budget. The $115–135B AI capex line is being partially financed by the middle-manager headcount. This is the Token-as-Labor-Unit thesis arriving in numbers.

Here is my call for the week: the Wednesday expiry does not get extended in its current form. Either Iran breaks it with a symbolic Hormuz event (third in the cycle), or the US quietly moves to a longer conditional framework. Either way, oil prices the 3rd closure before it happens. If you are sizing India fuel-excise-cut exposure (OMCs, paint, air) — size it now while WTI is only at $89, not after a test of $100.

The question I am sitting with: at what point does the market price Canada's decoupling as a permanent premium on CAD and a discount on TSX resource names? I think within 90 days.

The Long Game
The slow-burn trend to track this year is sovereign AI stacks. Canada's $890M SCIP, France's Mistral-anchored build, the UAE's G42 investment, India's IndiaAI mission, the UK's DSIT compute commitments — five G20 countries are now simultaneously building non-US-non-China AI infrastructure. This is not a bubble; it is decoupling expressed in silicon. The question for 2027 is whether these sovereign stacks interoperate (creating a de-facto "third bloc" in AI) or fragment into national walled gardens. Carney's Canada has chosen a side early. Watch whether Cohere's SCIP alignment becomes a blueprint others copy — or whether the US responds by weaponizing CHIPS Act precedent against sovereign buyers of its hardware.
