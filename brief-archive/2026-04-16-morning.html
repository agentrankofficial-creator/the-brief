India · Energy ☢️ Generational ACT
India's Fast Breeder Reactor Goes Critical. Bhabha's 75-Year Plan Is Unlocking.

On April 6, the 500 MWe Prototype Fast Breeder Reactor (PFBR) at Kalpakkam, Tamil Nadu achieved first criticality — 15 years behind schedule, but a monumental milestone. The reactor uses plutonium-uranium MOX fuel cooled by liquid sodium, and breeds more fissile material than it consumes. This is Stage 2 of India's three-stage nuclear program, unlocking the path to Stage 3: running India's massive thorium reserves (25% of global total). Russia has the only other commercial fast breeder reactor in operation.

SO WHAT (India): Nuclear currently provides only 3% of India's 8.7 GW installed capacity. This milestone doesn't flip the switch overnight — the PFBR must still go through months of controlled testing before commercial operations. But if a fleet of FBRs follows, India's long-term energy independence calculus changes dramatically: 25% of global thorium reserves becomes the world's largest sovereign energy endowment. SO WHAT (Canada): India previously imported uranium from Canada (multi-billion dollar agreements). A successful thorium-path reduces India's long-term Canadian uranium dependency. Canada's uranium sector (Cameco) has a 10-15 year window before this becomes materially relevant.
ACTION: Watch for PFBR commercial operations announcement (estimated 6-12 months). The second milestone to track is government budget allocation for fleet replication — if India funds 5-10 FBRs, the nuclear supply chain (BHEL, NPCIL, L&T heavy engineering) becomes a multi-decade growth story.
💭 India's nuclear program has a poor track record on timelines — the PFBR was supposed to be operational in 2010. Achieving criticality is the science; proving the economics and scaling to a commercial fleet are entirely different challenges. Japan, France, and the US all tried and failed at breeder reactor commercialization.
Read original → Gmail source

Bhabha's three-stage plan (1954): Stage 1 — Pressurized Heavy Water Reactors (PHWRs) using natural uranium, producing plutonium as byproduct (complete, 22 reactors operational). Stage 2 — Fast Breeder Reactors (FBRs) using plutonium, breeding more fuel than consumed (just achieved criticality). Stage 3 — Thorium-based reactors using U-233 bred from thorium, unlocking India's vast reserves (10-20 years away).

The AI-energy connection: India's AI data center ambitions require roughly 2-5 GW of reliable baseload power within this decade. Intermittent solar and wind can't provide this reliably. Nuclear — including eventually a fleet of FBRs — is the only credible baseload solution for AI sovereignty. India's PFBR success connects directly to whether India can credibly host large-scale AI infrastructure domestically.

Canada angle deeper: Cameco (TSX: CCO) is the world's largest publicly traded uranium company. India's long-term uranium import dependency has been a revenue tailwind. The PFBR path creates a 15-20 year transition window — meaningful but not urgent. More immediately, Cameco benefits from global nuclear renaissance demand driven by AI data center power needs.
