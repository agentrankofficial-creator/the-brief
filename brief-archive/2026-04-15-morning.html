After 21 hours of negotiations in Islamabad personally led by VP JD Vance, no deal was reached with Iran. Trump told reporters Iran had "reached out to seek a peace deal," which Iran did not immediately confirm. WTI crude fell 6.83% today to $92.31 — markets are pricing in a deal. SO WHAT: India imports ~85% of crude oil and is acutely exposed. The rupee is already at ₹93.08/USD. A prolonged Hormuz disruption adds ~₹8,000–12,000 crore/month to India's import bill per $10 crude price move. This feeds directly into CAD widening, rupee weakness, and SEBI-level anxiety about FII outflows. The Indian stock market (Nifty -0.86% today) is already pricing this in — but not fully.
ACTION: Watch for the peace-deal announcement — markets will spike 2–3% in a single session when it comes. WTI likely falls to low $80s. For India: watch RBI policy response to rupee slide and any fiscal response to fuel subsidies. If the blockade extends beyond May, India's Q1 FY27 GDP growth forecast of 7.2% becomes questionable.
💭 Bear case: Iran peace signals could be tactical — buying time to reposition militarily. The 2-week ceasefire that preceded this blockade shows how quickly things can collapse. 90 days of Hormuz disruption would eliminate 1B+ barrels and produce the largest oil supply shock since the 1973 Arab embargo.

The full oil math: The conflict has been ongoing for nearly two months. 90% of Iran's economic activity flows through the Strait. Saxo Bank's Head of Commodity Strategy Ole Hansen says this started as an inflation shock but risks morphing into a global growth constraint. Beyond crude: LNG, fertilizers, petrochemicals, and chip supply chains all run through Hormuz-adjacent routes.

Why markets are wrong (or right for now): The S&P rallied over 1% on peace-deal hopes. That's rational if a deal comes. But the 400M barrels already destroyed can't be unblocked overnight — Saudi Aramco can increase output by 1–2M bpd maximum, and that takes 90 days to flow through to refiners. The price relief from a peace deal will be real but partial.

India's fiscal math: Every $10 increase in crude costs India roughly $12–15 billion annually in additional import spending. At sustained $95 crude, India's current account deficit widens by ~0.5% of GDP. That forces RBI to either let the rupee slide further (inflationary) or defend it (depletes reserves). Neither is costless.

The WTO's 28-Year Digital Trade Shield Just Expired — India's $250B IT Export Industry Is Suddenly Naked

On March 30, 2026, at the WTO's 14th Ministerial Conference in Yaoundé, Cameroon, the e-commerce moratorium — which since 1998 has prevented countries from imposing customs duties on digital transmissions (software, streaming, cloud services) — expired for the first time in 28 years. Brazil and Turkey blocked the extension, with Brazil using the moratorium as leverage in a long-running agricultural grievances fight. The US, which wanted a permanent extension, "left Yaoundé empty-handed." Finshots (morning@finshots.in) provided the best plain-English breakdown of the implications. The legal certainty that businesses have relied on for nearly three decades is gone — countries can now potentially impose tariffs on digital imports, though they'd need domestic legislation first.

SO WHAT: India is uniquely exposed from both sides. If India tries to impose digital tariffs on imported services (Netflix, AWS, Google Workspace) to protect domestic players, other countries can retaliate against India's $250B+ IT services and BPO exports. That's an asymmetric vulnerability — India earns far more from digital exports than it would collect from digital import tariffs. India's IT industry (TCS, Infosys, Wipro, HCL) built its global dominance in a world where cross-border digital trade was frictionless. That world may be ending.
ACTION: Watch for India's position in the alternative plurilateral Agreement on Electronic Commerce (which 66 WTO members signed at Yaoundé). If India joins, it gets trade rule coverage. If not, it operates in a grey zone where bilateral tariff threats become a negotiating weapon others can use against Indian IT firms. The next 90 days of WTO Geneva talks will determine this.
💭 Digital tariffs won't appear overnight — countries need domestic legislation, and most are not prepared to move quickly. The moratorium's lapse is more symbolic than immediately operational. India's IT exports are protected by bilateral investment treaties and service-level agreements that don't depend on the WTO framework alone.
Read original → Gmail source

The Finshots explanation: The 1998 moratorium was born because the WTO had no framework for digital trade — it was built for a world of physical goods. A downloaded movie doesn't cross a border in a container ship. When software or music went digital, governments couldn't agree whether it was a "good" or a "service," so they agreed not to tax it at all while they figured it out. They never figured it out. Meanwhile the global digital economy grew to $30+ trillion around this rule.

The India paradox: India's IT giants earn from exporting digital services to the US, EU, and UK. If those countries retaliate against Indian IT in response to India imposing digital tariffs, the calculus is clearly negative for India. Yet India also has a domestic digital industry (Meesho, Jio, Ola) that competes against global platforms. The interests are in direct conflict, which is why India has historically been ambivalent about the moratorium.
