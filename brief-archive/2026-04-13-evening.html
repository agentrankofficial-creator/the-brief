ANALYSIS

Dalio has published a detailed 13-step world war escalation framework, and places us at Step 9 — simultaneous multi-theater conflicts with hardening alliance structures. He points to active shooting wars (Ukraine-Russia, Israel-Gaza-Lebanon-Syria, Yemen-Sudan, US-Israel-Iran) alongside non-shooting economic, trade, and technology wars. His quote: "The monetary order, some domestic political orders, and the geopolitical world order are breaking down." He draws parallels to 1913–14 and 1938–39, with a US-China confrontation over Taiwan as the most consequential risk, peaking around 2028.

Then it got worse. US VP JD Vance led 21 hours of marathon talks in Islamabad — the highest-level US-Iran meeting since the 1979 revolution — and they collapsed. Iran's demands: control of the Strait of Hormuz, release of frozen assets, war reparations, and a regional ceasefire. Vance called Washington's proposal a "final and best offer." Then Trump threatened to blockade the Strait of Hormuz entirely (CNN, NPR — April 12). Two supertankers had already U-turned. Oil is in full panic mode.

Global market reaction this week: Sensex -0.36%, Nifty Bank -1.39%. But globally: S&P 500 +3.56%, Nikkei 225 +7.84%, FTSE 100 +1.55%, Shanghai +2.74%. Capital is flowing OUT of India and INTO Japan, US, and North Asia. The divergence is the story.

SO WHAT

A Hormuz blockade would shut off ~20% of global oil supply. This isn't a tail risk anymore — it's a stated policy threat from the US President. Dalio's Step 9 framework gives you the macro lens: this is not one conflict, it's a system of conflicts reinforcing each other. Defense, energy infrastructure, gold, and hard assets are the hedge. Japan's +7.84% week tells you where the smart money is rotating.

WHAT TO DO
  • Audit EM exposure — capital is actively rotating to Japan, Taiwan, South Korea
  • Hormuz blockade risk = oil shock scenario. Energy longs, airline/transport shorts
  • Gold is Dalio's explicit recommendation (he's been saying this since Feb 2026)
  • Defense sector is a structural multi-year play across US, India, Europe
  • Watch for Iran's response to "final and best offer" within 48-72 hours
CONTRARIAN VIEW

Trump's Hormuz blockade threat may be negotiation leverage, not intent. Iran's foreign ministry said "no one expected a deal in one day" — diplomatic language for "we're still talking." Pakistan is actively mediating and urged both sides to continue upholding the existing ceasefire. Vance explicitly left the door open, calling Washington's proposal a "final and best offer" — which means it's still on the table. The 21 hours of face time was itself a breakthrough (highest-level US-Iran contact since 1979). Markets may be pricing in the worst case while the base case is a messy, extended negotiation that eventually produces a framework — not a blockade.
